Robinson Crusoe:Daniel Defoe:05/12/1719:By the end of the 19th century, no book in English literary history had enjoyed more editions, spin-offs and translations. Crusoe’s world-famous novel is a complex literary confection, and it’s irresistible.
Gulliver’s Travels:Jonathan Swift:12/24/1726:A satirical masterpiece that’s never been out of print, Jonathan Swift’s Gulliver’s Travels comes third in our list of the best novels written in English

Frankenstein:Mary Shelley:06/12/1818:Mary Shelley’s first novel has been hailed as a masterpiece of horror and the macabre.

Wuthering Heights:Emily Brontë:01/12/1847:Emily Brontë’s windswept masterpiece is notable not just for its wild beauty but for its daring reinvention of the novel form itself.
Vanity Fair:William Thackeray:06/21/1848:William Thackeray’s masterpiece, set in Regency England, is a bravura performance by a writer at the top of his game.

Moby-Dick:Herman Melville:11/12/1851:Wise, funny and gripping, Melville’s epic work continues to cast a long shadow over American literature.
Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland:Lewis Carroll:09/11/1865:Lewis Carroll’s brilliant nonsense tale is one of the most influential and best loved in the English canon.
The Moonstone:Wilkie Collins:09/12/1868:Wilkie Collins’s masterpiece, hailed by many as the greatest English detective novel, is a brilliant marriage of the sensational and the realistic.
Little Women:Louisa May Alcott:12/13/1868:Louisa May Alcott’s highly original tale aimed at a young female market has iconic status in America and never been out of print.
Middlemarch:George Eliot:09/24/1871:This cathedral of words stands today as perhaps the greatest of the great Victorian fictions.

Three Men in a Boat:Jerome K Jerome:17/11/1889:Jerome K Jerome’s accidental classic about messing about on the Thames remains a comic gem.

New Grub Street:George Gissing:14/11/1891:George Gissing’s portrayal of the hard facts of a literary life remains as relevant today as it was in the late 19th century.

Dracula:Bram Stoker:01/01/1897:Bram Stoker’s classic vampire story was very much of its time but still resonates more than a century later.

Sister Carrie:Theodore Dreiser:16/12/1900:Theodore Dreiser was no stylist, but there’s a terrific momentum to his unflinching novel about a country girl’s American dream.
Kim:Rudyard Kipling:09/12/1901:In Kipling’s classic boy’s own spy story, an orphan in British India must make a choice between east and west.
